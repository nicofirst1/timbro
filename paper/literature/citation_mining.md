# Citation mining (backward snowball sample)

## What this is

A backward citation-mining (snowball sampling) pass over the reference lists of the 21 High/Medium-relevance papers already in `paper/literature.md`'s Matrix table. The goal is not to re-review those 21 papers (see `literature.md` / `literature_full.md` / `literature_review.md` for that) but to find which *other* works — not yet in our tracker — are cited most often across their bibliographies. High cross-citation frequency (bibliographic coupling) is a signal of foundational or must-read works we've missed and should pull in via further snowball search.

**Methodology.** For each of the 21 tracker papers, its reference list was pulled via the Semantic Scholar API (`/paper/{id}/references`), with arXiv-HTML or arXiv-PDF bibtex/reference-section parsing as a fallback when Semantic Scholar rate-limited (HTTP 429) or the paper wasn't indexed there. Each reference was normalized to `Title | Year | FirstAuthorLastName | arXivID_or_DOI_or_NA`. References were then deduplicated across all 20 successful bibliographies using arXiv ID or DOI as the authoritative key when present, falling back to a normalized-title match (lowercase, punctuation stripped, whitespace collapsed) when no ID was given. Works already in `literature.md`'s Matrix (by arXiv ID or title) were excluded — this table is candidates *not yet* on our list.

**Coverage caveat.** Of the 21 tracker papers targeted, 20 yielded usable reference lists. `preprints202604-1817` ("A Survey of Agent Skills for Foundation-Model Agents," Jinhao Shen et al.) failed: preprints.org returned HTTP 403 on both the manuscript page and the PDF download URL, and Semantic Scholar was rate-limited (HTTP 429) at the time. Its references could not be recovered by any fallback and it is excluded from this analysis entirely. **This is a partial citation graph, not a complete one** — it reflects only what 20 of our 21 tracked papers cite, not what the full literature cites. It will systematically miss anything published after those 20 papers, and anything from ecosystem silos those 20 papers don't touch (e.g. whatever `preprints202604-1817` alone would have surfaced).

**Scale.** 1,257 individual reference lines were parsed across the 20 files. After clustering (arXiv/DOI-first, title-fallback) they collapse to 956 distinct cited works; 9 of those are references to papers already in our own tracker (mostly the 20 source papers citing each other), leaving **947 unique candidate works not yet in `literature.md`**.

## Highest-value candidates

Judgment call on which rows above are actually on-topic for our RQ1 (linguistic dialects across ecosystems), RQ2 (adoption prediction from linguistic features), or RQ3 (execution outcomes from restyled instructions) — as opposed to generic ML/agent-benchmark citations that are widely cited simply because they're infrastructure everyone uses.

1. **SkillsBench** (2602.12670, count 11) — the single strongest candidate by far: an execution benchmark specifically for *how well agent skills work*, cited by 11/20 of our own tracker papers (more than half). Directly relevant to RQ3 and possibly RQ2 (a ready-made execution-outcome harness rather than one we'd have to build ourselves for the WS4 pilot).
2. **How Well Do Agentic Skills Work in the Wild: Benchmarking LLM Skill Usage in Realistic Settings** (2604.04323, count 4) — same family as #1: empirical execution-outcome measurement of skills "in the wild," directly RQ3-relevant.
3. **Agent Skills in the Wild: An Empirical Study of Security Vulnerabilities at Scale** (2601.10338, count 4) — large-scale empirical corpus study of skills (security angle, but the corpus-scale empirical methodology is the reusable part for RQ1/RQ2 corpus construction).
4. **SoK: Agentic Skills – Beyond Tool Use in LLM Agents** (2602.20867, count 5) — a "systematization of knowledge" survey specifically about agent skills; good candidate for RQ1 background/positioning, worth checking for a taxonomy that overlaps or conflicts with Ling/Cho framing.
5. **Agent Skills for Large Language Models: Architecture, Acquisition, Security, and the Path Forward** (2602.12430, count 4) — another dedicated agent-skills survey; background/positioning check for RQ1, and to make sure the "path forward" section hasn't already proposed a linguistic-features research direction that would need differentiation.
6. **Agent Skills from the Perspective of Procedural Memory: A Survey** (techrxiv, count 3) — third dedicated skills survey; same background-check rationale as #4/#5.
7. **Agent Skills Enable a New Class of Realistic and Trivially Simple Prompt Injections** (2510.26328, count 3) — demonstrates that skill-file *text content* directly drives agent behavior (a security framing of the same causal link our RQ3 pilot tests); worth citing as independent evidence that instruction text, not just structure, changes execution outcomes.
8. **Lost in the Middle: How Language Models Use Long Contexts** (TACL, count 4) — foundational NLP result on how instruction position/format affects what an LLM actually attends to; not skill-specific but directly supports the mechanism behind why linguistic/structural framing of a SKILL.md could plausibly change execution outcomes (RQ3 theoretical grounding).

**Foundational/generic citations, probably not worth reading individually:** ReAct, Reflexion, Toolformer, Voyager, CAMEL, Chain-of-Thought prompting, "A Survey of Large Language Models," "A Survey on LLM-based Autonomous Agents," SWE-bench, SWE-agent, WebArena, AgentBench, AppWorld — these are widely-cited general agent/LLM infrastructure and benchmark papers. They recur across nearly every cluster (skill-evolution papers, ecosystem-survey papers, and benchmark papers alike cite them for boilerplate background or as evaluation environments) rather than because they're specifically about agent-skill instruction text or linguistic features. Several (SWE-bench, WebArena, AppWorld) are candidate *task sources* for a WS4-style execution pilot, similar to how we're already using Terminal-Bench — worth keeping on a "possible pilot environment" shortlist even though they don't need a close read.

**A large adjacent cluster not on our critical path:** roughly 15-20 of the top-40 rows (SkillRL, SkillClaw, CoEvoSkills, SkillFlow, SkillWeaver, AutoSkill, EvoSkill, Memento, MemP, MemSkill, SkillForge, SkillX, CASCADE, Experience Compression Spectrum, Cua-skill, Agent Workflow Memory, Reinforcement Learning for Self-Improving Agent with Skill Library) are about **skill acquisition/evolution via reinforcement learning or self-improvement loops** — a distinct research thread from ours. They cluster heavily because gao2026, liu2026-skillsvote, preprints202605-1276, li-codeskill, and liang2026-skillnet are themselves survey/ecosystem papers that enumerate this entire sub-literature. None of them measure linguistic properties of skill text or its effect on adoption/execution — they're about *how skills get created or improved*, not *what form skill prose takes*. Low priority for a close read; useful only as evidence of how crowded the "skill evolution" sub-field has become (a related-work scoping note, not a citation we need to engage with deeply).

## Limitations

- **Extraction quality is uneven.** Some of the 20 source bibliographies came from clean Semantic Scholar JSON; others were recovered via arXiv-HTML or PDF/bibtex parsing as a fallback, which is more error-prone (OCR artifacts, truncated titles, wrong or swapped author fields — e.g. several rows above show a first-name string instead of a surname, or two different author strings for the same arXiv ID depending on which source paper's bibliography supplied it). Author fields especially should not be trusted without verification against the arXiv ID.
- **Title-based dedup is heuristic, not exact.** Works cited under an arXiv ID in one bibliography and without one (title-only) in another were merged only on an exact normalized-title match; near-duplicate titles with more than trivial wording differences would not merge and could appear as separate rows, inflating the apparent count of the "smaller" duplicate and deflating the true count of the merged work. Conversely, no fuzzy/generic-word merging was applied, so distinct papers that happen to share generic terms (e.g. two different "agent benchmark" papers) were kept separate, as intended — but this means some true duplicates with heavily rewritten titles may still be sitting as separate singleton rows in the long tail (the 804 count-1 entries were not manually audited for this).
- **This is a snowball sample, not a citation census.** It reflects only what 20 of our 21 tracked papers cite — it cannot surface anything published after those 20 papers, anything outside the citation neighborhoods those 20 papers sit in, or anything `preprints202604-1817` alone would have surfaced (that paper's references are simply missing from this analysis; see the coverage caveat above).
- **Excluding tracker matches is also heuristic.** The exclusion list was built by matching arXiv IDs and normalized titles against `literature.md`'s Matrix table; a small number of the source papers' self-citations to each other were caught and excluded this way, but any tracker entry cited under a materially different title (e.g. a preprint-to-camera-ready title change) could have been missed and would incorrectly appear in the ranked candidates above.
